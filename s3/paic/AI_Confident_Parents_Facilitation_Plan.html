Why we are here

We are not here to panic. We are here to think, clearly, with the people sitting next to us.

Something significant is underway. Not a smarter search engine, but a shift in what intelligence is worth, who holds it, and what our children do with their time when machines can reason, write, and create alongside them. Parents do not need to become technologists. They need to become confident, present, and clear.

"If AI can generate answers, our people must ask better questions. If AI can optimise systems, our people must uphold values. If AI can accelerate progress, our people must decide its direction."
Minister for Education Desmond Lee, MOE Committee of Supply Debate, 3 March 2026. This line is the spine of the morning. Read it aloud at the open and return to it at the close.

The Minister also named what schools will do, and by extension what families can mirror at home: students will learn about AI, learn to use AI, learn with AI, and, perhaps most important, learn beyond AI. AI, in his words, "is not infallible, nor can it replace the human spirit." Our job this morning is to put that sentence into a parent's hands.

The promise · what every parent leaves with

Three critical learnings. Head, hands, heart.

Everything in the room serves these three. If a parent remembers nothing else, they remember these, and they leave wanting to try.

01
Head · what AI is

The intelligence has to come from us.

AI is a tool that mirrors the person using it. It predicts, it does not understand. Without your context, your judgement, and your values, it gives you the average answer. The thinking is still ours, and still our child's.

02
Hands · how to engage

Sit beside, not in front, not away.

The move is guided autonomy. Not banning, not handing over. We explore together, we set a family agreement, and we are willing to be perfectly imperfect, getting it wrong alongside our child and learning out loud.

03
Heart · what stays human

Protect what AI cannot grow.

The struggle that builds a mind, the effort that earns confidence, the relationship at the dinner table. We learn beyond AI so our children add human value, and use AI to amplify it, never to replace it.

Facilitator note. Name these three at the open as a promise. Tick them off visibly as the morning lands each one: Block 1 lands the Head, Explorer Rooms and Olympics land the Hands, the Heart block lands the last.

Three frameworks, five principles

The scaffolding parents carry home.

Light enough to remember in a corridor conversation, sturdy enough to lean on at 10pm when an essay arrives written by a chatbot.

Framework 1

The 4 Learns

From MOE, reframed for the home.

About AI Use AI With AI Beyond AI

Know it, wield it, learn through it, and grow what it cannot. "Beyond" is the one that protects your child.

Framework 2

A · I · R

How to weigh any AI moment.

Augmentation. AI helps you decide. It should not decide for you.

Intelligence. The understanding comes from the human. Give context or get the average.

Responsibility. The choices, and the work, remain yours.

Framework 3

Both / And

Polarity thinking. Most AI questions are not problems to solve but tensions to hold.

Protect and prepare. Guide and release. Trust and verify. We do not pick a side. We manage the balance over time.

The five principles

Principle 1

Guided autonomy

Not control, not abdication. Hand over freedom in proportion to demonstrated judgement.

Principle 2

Perfectly imperfect

Model being a learner. Get it wrong in front of your child. Confidence is built by trying, not by knowing.

Principle 3

Verify, do not trust

AI is confident, not correct. Check it the way we teach children to check a stranger's directions.

Principle 4

Protect the struggle

Do not outsource the effort that grows the mind. Some friction is the lesson, not the obstacle.

Principle 5

Together, not alone

Connection over correction. The goal is a child who comes to you, not one who hides the screen.

Grouping metrics & room design

Who sits where, and why it matters.

Comfort, not age, is the organising metric. A confident parent next to a hesitant one is the single best teaching aid in the room. We design for that.

Metric 1 · Self-rated comfort (set at registration)

Asked on the sign-up form and confirmed by a lanyard dot on arrival. This assigns the Explorer Room for the hands-on block.

Metric 2 · Child's year level (relevance, not seating)

Captured on the form for the facilitator's awareness, surfaced in the conversation-starter takehome, not used to seat parents. Roughly: lower secondary parents worry about exposure and screen time, upper secondary parents worry about academic honesty and pathways. Acknowledge both.

Metric 3 · Olympics teams (deliberately mixed)

For the Prompt Olympics we break the comfort bands and rebuild mixed teams of five to six, one Navy and one Cream parent on every team. The confident parent becomes the typist, the hesitant parent becomes the director. This is where "perfectly imperfect" stops being a slogan.

Room maths

~16 tables

100 parents at 6 per table. Round tables, laptop friendly, power strips taped down on every second table.

Helpers

Three floating helpers, one anchored per Explorer Room, plus a tech-desk at the back for login trouble.

Signal

Lanyard dots

Colour-coded dots let helpers read the room at a glance and pair people quickly for the Olympics.

The signature challenge

Prompt Olympics · run sheet.

The most memorable thing parents do this term, and the cleanest proof that AI quality is shaped by the human using it.

Format

  • Mixed teams of 5–6. One device per team on the screen-share, the rest watching and directing.
  • Three rounds, 5 minutes each, 2 minutes to judge between.
  • Roles rotate: the least confident parent directs round 1.

Scoring · 3 points each

  • Specific — could this only be your family's answer?
  • Useful — would you actually send or use it?
  • Human-shaped — can we see the person behind the prompt?

The three round briefs

RoundBriefWhat it teaches
1 · UsefulPlan a Saturday in Singapore your real teenager would not roll their eyes at. Budget $50.Context beats cleverness. The team that describes their actual child wins.
2 · HonestTurn AI into a study partner that helps your child revise without doing the work for them.The line between help and cheating is a prompt you can write.
3 · HumanMake something that would make your child smile: a quiz on family in-jokes, a silly poem, a game.The irreplaceable bit is the relationship. AI just held the pen.

Facilitator note. Crown a winner per round, not overall. Three winners means three tables that leave proud. The leaderboard is theatre; the learning is the gap between teams.

The honest part · know before you need it

Guardrails for the home.

Delivered with care, not alarm. Parents cannot guard against what they have never heard named.

Watch closely

Companion chatbots

Most teenagers have already tried an AI companion, and many use one regularly. These are built to keep a lonely young person talking, agreeable and affirming by design, and some have failed to step back when a teenager showed real distress. Treat them as a wellbeing question, not a tech question. Keep the conversation, not the confiscation.

Settle early

Privacy is a habit

Whatever your child types may train a future model or sit on a server. The household rule is simple and teachable: no real names, no photos, no address, no secrets, no other people's information. If you would not put it on a postcard, do not paste it into a chatbot.

Teach the reflex

Confident, not correct

AI invents facts with a straight face. Teach one reflex: ask "how do you know that?" and check anything that matters against a second source. This is the same skill we want for the rest of their lives.

Hold the line

Protect the effort

The danger is not a wrong answer, it is a child who never wrestles with the question. Disclose AI use openly, do the thinking first, use AI to check and stretch, not to skip. If a child cannot explain what they handed in, AI did too much.

If a parent is worried about a child right now, the morning is not the place to diagnose. Point them to the school counsellor and, for after hours, national helplines. Note it warmly, move on, follow up privately.
