The frame

Leading in, with, and beyond AI.

Three questions tonight. How do we use it. How do we govern it. How do we keep a school that learns.

You already lead people through change. This change is different. It acts on its own. Reading a brief and deciding is no longer enough.

The premise

You cannot govern what you have only been briefed on.

A brief tells you what a tool did once. Building one shows you how it breaks, where it drifts, and what you must never let it do.

The national frame

The country already named the moves.

At the 2026 Committee of Supply debate, the goal for every student was set as four moves: learn about AI, use AI, learn with AI, and learn beyond AI. More than slogans, she said.

Her four name what students do. Ours name what leaders do. Same shape, one level up.

The posture

We PILOT it. We stay in command.

In command of the craft, like a pilot. We set the course, engage the systems, and answer for the flight, even on autopilot.

Inside the posture sit four aspects. Two for how you work the tool. Two for what the tool is for.

Co-Author AIthe machine

You learn from it, and it learns from you. Feed your judgement back in. Push its boundary out. Correct its bias.

Govern AIthe machine

Deploy, delegate, discern. Operate it and rule it. Discern is the verb that catches its bias.

Transform the Humanthe human

Supercharge thinking. Move the person past their own ceiling. The thing transformed is the person, not the model.

Stay Humanthe human

Guard the part no model reaches. The noticing, the mentoring, the presence, the values.

The machine pair serves the human pair. One pushes the person forward. One holds the core. None of the four sits inside AI's bounded, biased knowledge. Each takes a stance on its edge.

The loop

A tool answers once. An agent runs a loop.

The loop is what is new. It sets a goal, acts, checks its own work, and tries again until the work is done. Watch the same task two ways.

Task: make a thirty second clip of the water cycle.

    The loop caught its own mistake and fixed it. That is what you govern. Not one answer. A process that keeps running when you are not watching.

    The human part

    Get your hands dirty

    Now you build.

    Free accounts are fine. First pick how far to take it. Then pick how to work: a relay in pairs, or a solo build. Both leave you holding something real.

    First, pick how far to take it.

    Tier 1
    A skill
    A school-context skill from your public website.
    Tier 2
    A tool
    One staff job. A PD summary. A feedback helper.
    Tier 3
    A dashboard

    Then pick how to work.

    Twelve minutes. Three roles. Each person sends only a handful of messages. Keep agents, loops, and connectors on the presenter's account. Swap roles after, if time.

    A · Co-Author
    Maker
    1. Paste:I'm building a reusable writing skill. Ask me six questions, one at a time, to capture how I write parent letters: tone, things I always do, things I never do, length, structure, and one short example.
    2. Answer the six questions.
    3. Paste:Now write it as a SKILL.md: a name, a "when to use" line, my voice rules as bullets, the format, and my example. Keep it tight. Inform only. End every draft by asking if I want to say more.
    4. Copy the SKILL.md. Hand it to your User.
    B · Co-Author
    User
    1. Paste the Maker's SKILL.md, then:Use this skill. Draft a parent letter about the science fair on 14 March. Inform only, minimal words.
    2. Change one thing and watch it adapt while holding the voice:Update: a wet-weather plan has been added, indoors if it rains. Redo.
    C · Govern (optional)
    Governor
    1. Paste the draft, then:What in this is sensitive, and what should never be put into a public AI tool?
    2. Then:Now argue the strongest case against sending this at all, in five bullets.
    The point. Maker instructs. User launches. Governor oversees. That is PILOT, in twelve minutes. A skill made by one person works for another: capture judgement once, reuse it.

    Pick which is worth your next twenty minutes. Two doors, one forge. Both free, both in a browser.

    Build a Skill: five moves

    1. Name the task you repeat. Reports, feedback, a kind of letter.
    2. Write what good looks like, in one line.
    3. List what it must always do. List what it must never do.
    4. Add three words of your voice.
    5. Paste it into a Claude Project. Test it on a real case.
    Done when. A colleague can use it without asking you anything.

          house-voice SKILL.md
          
    ---
name: house-voice
description: Use whenever drafting any school communication to staff, parents, or students: letters, notices, memos, messages, report comments. Keeps one consistent voice and keeps it short.
---

# House voice

## When to use
Any time I ask you to write or rewrite something that will be sent to staff, parents, or students.

## Voice rules
- Write to inform, not to persuade. State the thing plainly.
- Minimal words. Cut anything that does not carry information.
- Warm and direct. Respect the reader's time.
- Verb-forward. Prefer "we will" and "please" over abstract nouns.
- Plain English. No jargon, no slogans, no filler.
- British spelling. No em-dashes; use commas, colons, or full stops.
- Belief before rule when a reason helps, but keep it to one line.
- Never invent facts, dates, names, or figures. If one is missing, leave a clearly marked blank like [DATE].

## Format
- Lead with the single most important fact.
- One short paragraph, or a few tight lines. Notices and SMS can be one or two sentences.
- Close with the action, if any: what to do, by when.

## Behaviour
- Default to inform-only and keep it short.
- After the draft, ask once: "Would you like to use this to communicate something more, for example reassurance, a request, or a thank you?"
- Do not add persuasion, emotion, or extra length unless I say yes.

## Example
Prompt: "Parent letter. Science fair moved from 14 to 21 March, same hall, same times."
Draft: "Dear families, the Science Fair has moved to Friday 21 March. The venue and times are unchanged: the school hall, 9am to 12pm. No action is needed. We look forward to seeing you there."
Then: "Would you like to use this to communicate something more, for example reassurance, a request, or a thank you?"
