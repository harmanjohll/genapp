## 1. What the research says

A tight synthesis. Sources are listed at the end. Use any line as a slide.

**The gap.** Around 60% of K-12 principals now use AI tools, yet fewer than 10% of schools have any AI policy or formal guidance (Kaufman et al., 2025; UNESCO, 2025). Leaders are already using a thing they have not yet learned to govern. That is the whole case for building, not just being briefed.

**The time.** One analysis reports teachers save roughly 44% of the time they spend on research, planning, and making materials once they use AI (SchoolAI, 2026, citing teacher-use data). The hours come back. The question is what you do with them.

**The misread.** A 2026 scoping review found AI is used most for relational tasks, but fits best on managerial and strategic ones (Kumar & Sarkar, 2026). Leaders reach for it to write the warm note; it is stronger at re-solving the timetable. Worth naming in the room, because it inverts the instinct.

**The confidence gap.** School leaders report more AI familiarity and optimism than their teachers (Kim, 2026). If you are ahead, your job is to bring people with you, not to assume they sit where you sit.

**Go first.** Senior leaders create safety by going first: using AI in the open, showing the learning, admitting the adjustments (Center for Creative Leadership, in Partners for Educational Leadership, 2026). The room watches what you do, not what you say.

**Lead learner.** Award-winning "digital principals" describe the shift as becoming the lead learner, and handing lower-level tasks to AI to free time for people (Frontiers in Education, 2026).

**Not one and done.** "It's not one more thing. It's a thought partner, to help us think more critically and do our work better." And: start small, iterate, build support over time, rather than write a policy and walk away (Amanda Bickerstaff, AI for Education).

**Govern the conditions.** A PRISMA review of 50 studies names the leader's real levers: privacy and consent, data literacy, fairness and explainability, and contract oversight, taught job-embedded rather than in a one-off (Lipsou, Keravnos & Eteokleous, 2026).

**The thesis.** The evidence points one way. Leaders build literacy by doing, govern the conditions rather than approve tools, model use in the open, and guard the human core. That is this session, in one line.

## 2. Quick wins (do in minutes, free)

Short tasks. Each lands in under five minutes on a free account. Pick two or three for the room, give the prompt, let them try one on their own work. The point is the first taste of "it did that for me."

1. **Decisions out of a thread.** Paste a long email chain.
   > Pull out every decision, who owns it, and the deadline. Flag anything still open.

2. **A notice in your voice.** Give three or four bullets.
   > Draft a short parent notice from these points. Warm, clear, under one screen. No filler.

3. **Same meaning, lower reading age.** Paste a dense policy paragraph.
   > Rewrite this so a Secondary 1 student understands it. Keep every fact. Short sentences.

4. **The one page you needed.** Paste a long report.
   > Give me a one-page brief. Lead with the three things that matter for a school leader. Cut the rest.

5. **An agenda that fits the clock.** State the goal and the time.
   > Build a 45-minute meeting agenda to decide [X]. Put a time on each item. Leave room to land it.

6. **Feedback that grows people.** Paste rough lesson-observation notes.
   > Turn these into feedback for the teacher: two strengths, one focus, one question. Specific, kind, no labels.

7. **A choice, laid out.** Name the options and what matters.
   > Make a simple decision table for [options] against [criteria]. Mark trade-offs. Do not pick for me.

8. **The awkward email, started.** Describe the situation in a sentence.
   > Draft three openings for a hard message to a parent about [situation]: one warm, one direct, one that buys time. I will choose.

## 3. Wow to show (you run it)

Presenter demos. These are the moments that change the room. A tag shows what each needs. You have two paid accounts, so run the agent-and-connector ones there.

1. **The loop, live.** Ask it to build a small working tool in front of them, for example a phases-of-the-moon explainer. Watch it write, run, check its own work, fix what broke, and finish. No code, no terminal.
   *Needs: free for a basic build; a paid account or Cowork for the full agent loop on a real file.*

2. **Themes from 200 replies.** Paste a couple of hundred anonymised survey responses.
   > Cluster these into themes. Count each. Surface the outliers. Then draft a half-page summary for leaders.
   The wow is analysis at a scale no one does by hand in a meeting.
   *Needs: free (paste the text). Strip names first.*

3. **A Project that answers in your school's voice.** Load a school-context skill and a few reference documents into a Project. Ask it a real question. It answers from your knowledge base, in your voice. Say the line: a Project with a skill is, in concept, an AIBot with a knowledge base and a system prompt. The move transfers to Pair and AIBots.
   *Needs: free (Projects), one set up in advance.*

4. **Prompt to a live link.** Go from a prompt to a hosted page in minutes. Show Co-Cher and SgSL as the proof: a teacher built them on a free account, in a browser, and shipped them. This is the headline. It says "you, this week."
   *Needs: free to build; a GitHub account to host (Segment 6).*

5. **Live data, pulled in.** Ask about "my calendar this week" or "this document" and watch it reach your real data through a connector, then act on it.
   *Needs: paid account plus a connector switched on. Use a paid login.*

6. **"I described it, it built it."** Play the chemistry-clip pipeline: you describe a clip, the agent writes the code, renders it, checks the frames, fixes what broke, and adds narration. Show the finished clip. The leap is the point: you stopped making each one and built the thing that makes them.
   *Needs: nothing live, show the artefact.*

## 4. Wow they can do (free, hands-on)

Every one of these works on a free account. The aim is that each person leaves having built, not watched.

1. **A one-screen interactive.** Build a single offline HTML page for one thing students get wrong. Try a wrong answer. Check the feedback teaches. (The "Build a Thing" path.)

2. **A reusable skill.** Write your standard once, voice, values, always, never, then hand it to a partner and watch it work for them. (The "Build a Skill" path.)

3. **The relay.** In threes: Maker builds a skill, User runs it, Governor stress-tests it. Twelve minutes, a handful of messages each. (Task Card D.)

4. **A working tool for a real job.** Build a small calculator or checker for something on your plate: a CCA points tally, a duty-roster gap checker, a co-curricular budget splitter. Ask for one offline HTML file.

5. **Your own house voice.** Paste three things you have written. Ask it to infer your rules and write them as a skill. Then draft something new with it and see if it holds.

## 5. Task cards (with exact prompts)

Hand these out. Each is self-contained. The prompts are ready to paste.

### Card A — Build a parent-letter skill
**Time:** 8 minutes. **Account:** free.
1. Paste:
   > I'm building a reusable writing skill. Ask me six questions, one at a time, to capture how I write parent letters: tone, things I always do, things I never do, length, structure, and one short example.
2. Answer the six.
3. Paste:
   > Now write it as a SKILL.md: a name, a "when to use" line, my voice rules as bullets, the format, and my example. Keep it tight. Inform only. End every draft by asking if I want to say more.
4. Open a Claude Project. Paste the SKILL.md into the project instructions. Test it on a real letter.
**Done when:** a colleague can use it without asking you anything.

### Card B — Build a hosted lesson tool
**Time:** 15 minutes. **Account:** free to build, GitHub to host.
1. Paste:
   > Build one self-contained HTML file. It must open offline and run on a phone. Topic: [your topic]. Year level: [level]. The one thing a student must do: [the interaction]. Wrong idea to target: [misconception]. Rules: one interaction; show the task before the answer; a wrong answer gets feedback that teaches; end on a clear done state; no login, no internet once open.
2. Open it. Try a wrong answer. Check the feedback.
3. Host it (Segment 6). Share the link.
**Done when:** a student opens it on a phone and knows what to do.

### Card C — Themes from a survey
**Time:** 6 minutes. **Account:** free.
1. Remove names from the responses.
2. Paste them, then:
   > Cluster these into themes. Count how many fall under each. Surface anything that does not fit. Then draft a half-page summary for school leaders, with the one action each theme points to.
**Done when:** you have a brief you would actually send.

### Card D — The relay (in threes)
**Time:** 12 minutes. **Account:** free. Keep agents, loops, and connectors on the presenter's account.
- **A, Maker.** Use Card A's prompts to build a SKILL.md. Hand it on.
- **B, User.** Paste the skill, then:
  > Use this skill. Draft a parent letter about the science fair on 14 March. Inform only, minimal words.
  Then change one fact:
  > Update: a wet-weather plan has been added, indoors if it rains. Redo.
- **C, Governor.** Paste the draft, then:
  > What in this is sensitive, and what should never be put into a public AI tool?
  Then:
  > Now argue the strongest case against sending this at all, in five bullets.
**The point:** Maker instructs, User launches, Governor oversees. That is the posture, in twelve minutes.

## 6. Ship it: host on GitHub Pages

Free. Gets a public link anyone can open. Short and sharp, give them this as-is.

1. **Make an account.** Go to github.com. Sign up. Free is enough.
2. **New repository.** Click **New**. Name it (for example `my-tools`). Set it to **Public**. Click **Create repository**.
3. **Add your file.** Click **Add file**, then **Upload files**. Upload your HTML. It must be named **`index.html`**. Click **Commit changes**.
4. **Turn on Pages.** Open **Settings**, then **Pages** (under "Code and automation"). Under **Source**, choose **Deploy from a branch**. Under **Branch**, pick **main** and **/ (root)**. Click **Save**.
5. **Open it.** Wait one to two minutes. Go to `https://YOUR-USERNAME.github.io/my-tools`. Share that link.

**If the page is blank:** the file must be named `index.html`, it must sit at the root (not in a folder), and the repository must be **Public**. Give it a few minutes, or check the **Actions** tab for the build.

**Tip for the room:** GitHub can copy a starter template into someone's account in one click, so they replace `index.html` and skip the typing.

## 7. Free vs paid: what needs which

**Free covers every participant task.** Drafting, rewriting, summarising, analysing pasted text, building HTML tools, building skills, and using Projects all work on a free account. Plan the hands-on around free.

**Paid adds the live-agent moments.** More usage, fuller Projects, Connectors that reach live data (Drive, mail, calendar), and the workspace agents (Cowork, Code) that act on real files and tools, with longer runs. Use your two paid accounts for the demos in Segment 3: build-and-ship at full speed, live data through a connector, and the agent working on a real file.

**Governance line, repeated:** free Claude is for open work only. Student and staff data stay on the government platforms. Decide what data goes where before you build, not which tool is cleverer.
