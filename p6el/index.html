The 5 parts of a great story

Every awesome PSLE composition has the same 5 parts. Think of it as a roller-coaster ride. We climb up, reach the top, then come down.

1. Introduction
2. Rising Action
3. Climax
4. Falling Action
5. Conclusion

1. Introduction [start]

What it is: The opening of your story. Pull the reader in and set the scene.

  • Start with a hook: dialogue, a sound, an action, or a strong feeling.
  • Tell us who, where, when — but don't list them like a shopping list.
  • Hint at the trouble that's coming.

Boring: My name is Tom. I am 12 years old. One day, I went to the park with my friends.

Exciting: "Last one to the swings is a rotten egg!" Marcus yelled, sprinting ahead. I laughed and chased him, not knowing this would be the strangest afternoon of my life.

2. Rising Action [up]

What it is: The build-up. Things start to go wrong, or get more exciting.

  • Add 2 or 3 events that lead to the big moment.
  • Make the reader feel something is about to happen.
  • Use the 5 senses: what did you see, hear, smell, taste, feel?

Example: The sky darkened. A cold wind whipped through the trees, sending leaves spiralling around our feet. Somewhere far off, thunder grumbled like an angry giant. Marcus stopped laughing. "We should go home," he whispered.

3. Climax [!]

What it is: The most exciting part. The biggest moment of the story.

  • This is where the problem peaks.
  • Use short sentences to make it tense. Like this. Bang. Run.
  • Slow down time — describe it in detail.

Example: A jagged bolt of lightning split the sky. CRACK! The old oak tree beside us groaned, then began to fall — straight towards Marcus. My heart hammered. I shoved him with all my strength. We tumbled into the wet grass as the tree slammed down behind us.

4. Falling Action [down]

What it is: Things start to calm down. The problem is being solved.

  • Show the aftermath — what happened right after the climax?
  • Show characters reacting (shaking, crying, laughing in relief).
  • Wrap up loose ends.

Example: For a long moment, neither of us moved. My ears rang. Marcus's hand was trembling in mine. Slowly, we crawled out from under the branches. A jogger had seen everything and was already calling for help. Sirens wailed in the distance.

5. Conclusion [end]

What it is: The ending. Leave the reader with a feeling or a lesson.

  • Show how the character has changed or what they learned.
  • End on a strong image, thought, or feeling.
  • Don't just write "And that was my day." — that's a wasted ending!

Weak: And that was the scariest day of my life. The end.

Strong: That night, I lay awake listening to the rain tap against my window. I would never look at a thunderstorm the same way again — and I would never forget how close I had come to losing my best friend.

Show, Don't Tell

Don't tell the reader how someone feels. Show it through their body, actions, and surroundings. The reader should feel what your character feels.

Rule of thumb: if you wrote "He was ___", try again. Show us instead.

Sample 1: A Frightening Experience

Introduction "Just one quick shortcut," I muttered, glancing at the dark alley behind the hawker centre. Mother had warned me a hundred times never to take it, but it was already 9 p.m. and my legs ached from a long day at school. I tightened the strap of my schoolbag and stepped into the shadows, never imagining what was waiting for me there.

Rising Action The alley smelled of rotting fruit and damp cardboard. A flickering streetlamp buzzed somewhere overhead, throwing jumpy shadows on the brick walls. My footsteps echoed strangely — too loud, somehow. Then I heard it. Another set of footsteps, slow and heavy, falling in time with mine. I quickened my pace. So did the footsteps. My mouth turned dry as paper.

Climax Suddenly, a hand clamped down on my shoulder. I screamed. My schoolbag slipped from my arm and crashed onto the pavement, books spilling everywhere. I spun around, fists raised, my heart pounding so hard I could feel it in my ears.

Falling Action "Wei! Why you running so fast?" It was Uncle Lim, the noodle seller from the hawker centre. He was puffing, holding out my water bottle with a worried frown. "You drop this, lah." My knees turned to jelly. I let out a shaky laugh and bent down to gather my books, my hands still trembling.

Conclusion Uncle Lim walked me home that night, scolding me gently the whole way. As I climbed into bed, I promised myself I would never take that alley again. Some shortcuts, I had learned, are not worth the fright.

Sample 2: An Act of Kindness

Introduction The rain had been falling for hours by the time I left the library. I clutched my thin jacket around me and stared glumly at the flooded pavement. My bus was in five minutes, and I had no umbrella. That was when I noticed her — an elderly lady sitting on the bench, soaked to the skin, hugging a plastic bag of groceries.

Rising Action I hesitated. My bus would arrive any moment. If I missed it, I would be late for dinner and Mother would be furious. The lady coughed — a deep, rattling cough that shook her whole body. Her lips were turning a faint shade of blue. I looked at my watch. I looked at the lady. I looked at my watch again.

Climax Before I could change my mind, I darted over and pulled off my jacket. "Auntie, please use this," I said, draping it around her bony shoulders. Her eyes, milky with age, widened in surprise. Just then, my bus rumbled past in a spray of dirty water. Gone. I bit my lip hard.

Falling Action The auntie's wrinkled hand found mine and squeezed it tightly. "Thank you, child," she whispered, her voice cracking. We waited together for the next bus. When it finally arrived, I helped her on board and made sure she had a seat. She waved at me through the foggy window as the bus pulled away.

Conclusion I was twenty minutes late for dinner. I was cold, wet, and a little hungry. But as I walked home through the drizzle, I felt warmer than I had in a very long time. Sometimes, I realised, the smallest kindness can fill you up more than a hot meal ever could.
